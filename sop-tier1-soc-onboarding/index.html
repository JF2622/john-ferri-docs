Tier 1 SOC Onboarding SOP for Tooling and Access

Owner: SOC Lead / Operations

Last Updated: 2026-01-15

Audience: New Tier 1 Analysts and SOC Interns

Purpose:

This SOP details the step-by-step process to provision access and confirm readiness for Tier 1 SOC analysts to begin monitoring and triage on Day 1.

Audience:

  • Tier 1 SOC Analysts (New Hires)
  • SOC Team Leads who onboard analysts
  • IT and Identity administrators supporting access requests

Prerequisites:

Access and accounts

Before Day 1, confirm the following are completed:

  • The corporate SSO account was created and you can sign in successfully.
  • MFA was enrolled and is working on your approved device.
  • You have been added to the appropriate RBAC group(s) for “SOC Tier 1” (or equivalent).
  • VPN access was approved (if you are remote or hybrid) and you can connect without errors.
  • The ticketing system access was provisioned with create, view, and comment permissions.
  • The monitoring platform access is provisioned (read-only access is sufficient for Day 1).
  • You have access to the SOC communication channel and the escalation contact list.

Workstation and Connectivity

  • The company-managed workstation has been issued and you can log in locally.
  • The required browsers are installed and up to date.
  • You can reach required internal resources, such as the Intranet and the documentation portal while you are on the office network or the approved VPN.
  • Your time has been synchronized, with an accurate system clock, to prevent authentication and log-correlation issues.

Day 1 Checklist:

  1. Confirm SSO login and MFA enrollment.
    • Verification: Successfully sign into the corporate portal twice without being locked out.
  2. Confirm VPN Access and network reachability.
    • Verification: Connect to the VPN and reach internal resources, such as the Intranet homepage.
  3. Request and confirm SOC group membership (Tier 1 role).
    • Verification: The identity portal should show group membership or your manager should confirm your approval.
  4. Access the ticketing system and confirm you can view assigned queues.
    • Verification: Ensure that you can open a ticket and add a comment.
  5. Access the monitoring platform and confirm read-only visibility.
    • Verification: Ensure you can view dashboards and search logs.
  6. Complete a baseline "first alert" walkthrough with your lead.
    • Verification: Ensure you can document one test alert triage in a practice ticket/template.
  7. Verify escalation contacts and communication channels.
    • Verification: Ensure you can access SOC channels and that you have the escalation list.
  8. Document the completion of this process in an onboarding checklist.
    • Verification: Confirm that the checklist is marked complete and that your manager acknowledges it.

Escalation Criteria (Tier 1 → Tier 2/Lead)

Escalate when any of the following are true:

  • High Potential Impact: privileged accounts, production systems, large user populations, or regulated data
  • Credible Signs of Compromise: valid IOC match, repeated failed logins followed by success, impossible travel, or suspicious MFA behavior
  • Persistence or Spread: repeated alerts for the same entity in a short time window, or multiple related alerts across hosts/users
  • Unable to Verify Benign Cause Quickly: after reasonable triage (e.g., 15–30 minutes), you still cannot explain the activity
  • Policy Requires Escalation: severity threshold met, VIP/executive account involved, or “must escalate” playbook category

What to Include in the Escalation Note:

  • Alert name/type, timestamp range, and impacted user/host
  • What you observed (2–5 bullets)
  • What you already checked (logs queried, tickets referenced, change windows)
  • Your current assessment (suspected false/true positive and confidence level)
  • Recommended next action (containment request, deeper investigation, contact system owner)

Troubleshooting:

Symptom Likely Cause Quick Fix / Next Step Escalate To
Unable to log in to SSO (invalid password or account locked) Password not set, account not activated, lockout from multiple attempts Use password reset process; wait for lockout timer if applicable; retry once IT Service Desk / IAM
MFA fails (codes rejected, prompts not arriving) Wrong time on device, enrollment incomplete, incorrect method selected Verify device time sync; re-enroll MFA method; try backup method if available IT Service Desk / IAM
VPN connects but internal sites/tools don’t load Split-tunnel policy, DNS issue, wrong VPN profile Disconnect/reconnect; verify correct profile; flush DNS and retry Network Support / IT
“Access denied” to monitoring platform or empty dashboards RBAC group not assigned, permission propagation delay Confirm you’re in the SOC Tier 1 group; wait 15–30 minutes after approval; retry SOC Lead / IAM
Can’t view or update ticket queue Wrong project/queue permissions Confirm assigned queue/project; request correct role; test by commenting on a sample ticket SOC Lead / Ticketing Admin

Glossary:

  • SOC - Security Operations Center: This is a central location that uses a team of people, a set of processes, and an array of technological tools to continuously monitor, analyze, and respond to cybersecurity incidents and threats. It assists in improving an organization's overall security posture.

  • SIEM - Security Information and Event Management system: This is a cybersecurity solution that collects large amounts of data and analyzes activity from a wide array of devices, servers, and users across an organization, consolidating this information into one unified platform that grants an organization a comprehensive view of their cybersecurity posture. A SIEM helps a SOC with quickly and successfully detecting, investigating, and responding to security incidents.

  • Alert: A real-time notification from a security tool, such as a SIEM, that signals that there is suspicious activity or a possible threat (examples: malware, unauthorized logins, policy violations) that must be investigated to verify whether it is an actual security incident or noise.

  • Triage: This is an important process early on in the cyber incident response process, in which personnel and security tools will analyze, classify, and prioritize incoming security alerts and incidents to separate real threats from noise and allow resources to be concentrated on the most urgent events, similar to emergency medicine.

  • False Positive: This is a false alarm, also called noise, when an alert is generated by a security tool, such as a SIEM, incorrectly flagging normal activity as a threat or vulnerability.

  • False Negative: This is when a security system, such as a SIEM, fails to detect a threat, attacker, or vulnerability, instead labeling the activity as in question as normal. This allows malicious activity to continue without detection, potentially causing damage.

  • True Positive: This is a situation in which a security tool, such as a SIEM, correctly identifies a security threat or malicious activity, triggering an alert for a SOC analyst to investigate.

  • True Negative: This is a situation in which a security tool, such as a SIEM, correctly determines that there is currently no malicious activity, vulnerability, attack, or threat. No alert is generated, and normal activity can continue without issue.

  • IOC - Indicator of Compromise: This is a clue that there is a past or ongoing security breach. This clue can take many forms, ranging from a suspicious IP to a strange file hash or unexpected network traffic. An IOC can help analysts detect malware, unauthorized access, or data theft.

  • Escalation: This is the formal process of moving a cybersecurity alert or incident from a response by a lower-tier analyst (Tier 1, for example) to a more specialized team (such as an incident response team or a management team), when the situation exceeds the abilities of the initial responders, requires deeper investigation, or becomes a major business risk. There is often an escalation matrix to manage this process, delineating clear roles, triggers, and timelines.

  • Playbook: A playbook is a step-by-step, detailed guide or automated workflow that allows for consistent, efficient responses to certain kinds of cybersecurity threats. A playbook defines who does what, when, and with which tools, helping to detect, analyze, contain, and remediate cyber incidents.

  • RBAC - Role-Based Access Control: This is when access permissions are assigned to job roles instead of individuals. This ensures that analysts will only access tools and data that are needed for their job, helping to enforce the principle of least privilege, reduce risk, simplify management, and improve compliance.

  • MFA - Multi-Factor Authentication: This is a layered form of cyber defense that requires users to provide two or more different types of verification methods, such as a password and a code from an authenticator app or a text message, to prove their identity. MFA can reduce the risk of unauthorized access, even when passwords are stolen.
